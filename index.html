The Beginning

It started with a conversation I couldn't have

Last semester, my college placed a deaf student — I'll call him Rohit — in a few of my classes. Watching him navigate lectures, office hours, and lab sessions without an interpreter present most of the time was eye-opening in the worst way. He'd type slowly on his phone, show the screen to professors, and wait. The lag in that communication wasn't just inconvenient — it was isolating. I kept thinking: surely there's a better way to fix this with technology.

So when the Vision Possible hackathon dropped in my inbox with the theme of building real-time AI agents, my idea was immediate. I wanted to build something that could watch hand signs and translate them into spoken words — in real time, with under 500ms delay. Not a research demo. An actual tool someone like Rohit could use sitting across a professor.

That project became SignBridge. And Vision Agents by Stream became the backbone that made it possible without a six-person team or an expensive GPU cluster.

The thing about building for accessibility is that "good enough" isn't good enough. The latency has to be invisible. The accuracy has to be trustworthy. It has to actually work in messy real-world conditions. — What I kept reminding myself at 2 AM on Day 1
